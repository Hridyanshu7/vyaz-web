# Vyaz — Pitch Deck Answers

*For Accel, Elevation, Sequoia, Blume*

---

## 1. What is the problem?

**The world's knowledge is locked in books that nobody finishes.**

- 60% of books purchased are never completed. The average reader finishes 4 books/year but buys 12.
- The people who need books most — professionals, founders, students — are the ones with the least time to read them.
- Existing alternatives are broken:
  - **Summaries (Blinkist, SparkNotes)** — strip the depth. You get bullet points, not understanding. You can't ask a summary a question.
  - **Audiobooks (Audible)** — still 6-8 hours per book. Same time problem, different format.
  - **YouTube book reviews** — one person's monologue. No personalization, no interaction, no Q&A.
  - **Book clubs** — scheduling nightmare, half the members didn't finish, lowest-common-denominator discussion.

**The core pain:** There is no way to deeply understand a book — with the nuance, context, and personalization you need — in a time-efficient way.

---

## 2. Who are the users facing this problem?

**Two distinct personas, both underserved:**

### The Consumer (demand side)
- Professionals, founders, MBA students, lifelong learners
- They have growing reading lists and shrinking time
- They're self-aware about this — they don't lack interest, they lack hours
- They're willing to pay for time savings (already pay for Blinkist, Audible, MasterClass)
- **TAM signal:** Blinkist has 30M+ users paying $100/year for summaries. These users want MORE depth, not less — they just don't have an option.

### The Narrator (supply side)
- Bibliophiles who read 30-100+ books/year
- They've deeply internalized titles and love discussing them
- They already do this for free — at book clubs, dinner parties, on Reddit, on BookTok
- Many are underemployed knowledge workers — teachers, grad students, retired professionals
- They want: recognition, community, and eventually income from their expertise
- **TAM signal:** 50M+ Goodreads users with 500+ books shelved. 1M+ BookTok creators. This supply exists — it's just scattered and unmonetized.

### The intersection
The consumer wants personalized, interactive book knowledge in 30 minutes. The narrator has that knowledge and wants an audience. There is no marketplace connecting them. Vyaz is that marketplace.

## 3. What's the product? How does it solve the problem?

**Vyaz is a peer-to-peer marketplace where you book a 30-minute live session with someone who has deeply read the book you want to understand.**

### How it works:
1. **Pick a book** — browse the catalog with real ratings from Amazon + Goodreads, AI-generated review insights, genre tags
2. **Choose a narrator** — verified readers with ratings, reviews, and professional background. Pick the perspective you want (a founder narrating Zero to One vs a professor narrating it)
3. **Book a session** — 1:1 or group (up to 50). Google Calendar event auto-created with Meet link. Reminders fire automatically.
4. **Have the conversation** — structured templates guide the narrator (hot take → key ideas → Q&A → your takeaway), but the magic is the Q&A: you ask YOUR questions, not generic ones
5. **Walk away with understanding** — post-session takeaway card with your key insight. For chapter-wise sessions, a progress bar tracks your journey through the book.

### Why this is better than alternatives:

| | Vyaz | Blinkist | Audible | YouTube | Book Club |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Depth** | Full — narrator has read the whole book | Shallow — 15 min summary | Full but 6+ hrs | Varies | Varies |
| **Interactive** | Yes — live Q&A | No | No | No | Sort of |
| **Personalized** | Yes — adapts to your context | No | No | No | Partially |
| **Time** | 30 min | 15 min | 6-8 hrs | 15-30 min | 1-2 hrs + reading |
| **On-demand** | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | No — scheduled |

**The key differentiator:** You can ask a narrator "How does this apply to my SaaS startup?" You can't ask Blinkist that.

## 4. Why does this problem need to be solved now? Using this product?

### Why now:

**1. The knowledge economy demands it.**
The professional world runs on books — "What are you reading?" is the new "Where did you go to school?" But the gap between books people claim to have read and books they've actually understood is widening. The shame of not reading is growing, and so is the willingness to pay for alternatives.

**2. The creator economy has trained supply.**
BookTok has 200B+ views. BookTube has millions of creators. Goodreads has 150M+ users reviewing books. For the first time, there's a massive pool of people who are ALREADY narrating books for free — they just have no way to monetize it or do it 1:1. The supply side is pre-trained.

**3. Remote work normalized live video with strangers.**
Pre-COVID, paying to video-call a stranger about a book would feel bizarre. Post-COVID, it feels natural. Cambly (language tutoring via video) hit $100M+ ARR on this behavior shift. Vyaz rides the same wave for books.

**4. AI makes summaries free — human depth becomes premium.**
ChatGPT can summarize any book in 10 seconds. Free, instant, comprehensive. This commoditizes summaries to zero. What AI CAN'T do: have a Socratic conversation, adapt to your context, debate you, challenge your thinking, share a personal perspective shaped by experience. Human narration becomes the premium layer on top of free AI summaries.

### Why this product:

- **Not an AI tool** — AI summarizes. Vyaz connects you with a person. The value is in the human interaction, the Q&A, the personalized adaptation.
- **Marketplace, not content** — we don't create content. We connect demand with supply. This means zero content creation cost and infinite scalability of topics.
- **The Cambly model, proven** — Cambly proved that "pay for instant access to a person's time" works at scale ($100M+ ARR). Vyaz applies the same model to a different vertical: books instead of English.

## 5. Why can you solve this problem? What unfair advantages do you have?

### 1. Named after the advantage
Vyaz is named after **Vyasa** — the sage who narrated the Mahabharata in Indian mythology. India invented the format of oral knowledge transfer 3,000 years ago: a learned person narrates a text, explains it, and the audience asks questions. We're digitizing a cultural behavior that's deeply embedded in 1.4 billion people. No Western competitor can claim this cultural moat.

### 2. India-first, global by default
- India has the world's largest English-reading population under 30
- Indian professionals are the most time-starved and book-aspirational demographic globally
- Our narrator supply (educated English speakers willing to work for $5-15/hour) is globally competitive
- Same playbook as Cambly (US tutors → global learners) but inverted: Indian narrators → global listeners

### 3. Data moat from day one
Every session generates structured data:
- Which books are most demanded
- Which chapters are most discussed
- What questions listeners ask most
- Which narrator styles get best ratings
- What "aha moments" resonate
This data doesn't exist anywhere else — not on Goodreads, not on Amazon, not on any AI. It's a proprietary understanding of how people consume book knowledge.

### 4. Cross-platform book intelligence
We scrape and merge Amazon + Goodreads data for every book in the catalog — ratings, reviews, AI summaries, genre tags, pricing. This gives us the most comprehensive book profile page on the internet, richer than either platform individually. This becomes a discovery moat.

### 5. Network effects compound
More narrators → more titles covered → more listeners find what they want → more sessions booked → more narrators earn → more narrators join. The marketplace flywheel is self-reinforcing once ignited. The cold start is the hard part — after that, it compounds.

## 6. How do you solve the cold start problem? What's your GTM plan?

### Phase 0: Founder is the product (Week 1-2)
- Founder personally narrates 15-20 books, hosts every session
- Sidesteps the chicken-and-egg entirely — you don't need supply if you ARE the supply
- Every session is a learning opportunity: what do listeners actually want?
- **Target: 10 completed sessions**

### Phase 1: Recruit 10 narrators (Week 2-4)
- Personal network: friends who read voraciously
- Book club members: they already discuss books weekly
- Goodreads power reviewers: DM the top reviewers of our seed books
- BookTok/BookTube creators: "Be the first narrator on Vyaz"
- MBA students: they read business books constantly
- **Target: 10 narrators, 30+ titles covered**

### Phase 2: First 50 consumers (Week 3-6)
- **LinkedIn** (highest ROI): 3x/week posts — "I read [Book] so you don't have to" → CTA to Vyaz
- **Reddit**: r/books (22M), r/Entrepreneur (2M), r/suggestmeabook (3M) — genuine value, not spam
- **WhatsApp/Telegram**: MBA groups, startup founder groups, reading clubs
- **Book club partnerships**: "Members who didn't finish can catch up in 30 min with our narrators"
- **Target: 50 consumers, 20 sessions**

### Phase 3: Social proof flywheel (Week 4-8)
- Record session clips (60 seconds) → post on LinkedIn/Twitter
- Collect reviews → screenshot and share
- Narrator testimonials → "I explained Sapiens to 5 people this week"
- **One viral session clip on LinkedIn = 100+ signups overnight**

### Phase 4: Own the wedge (Week 6-12)
- **Wedge: Business/startup books for Indian professionals**
- Concentrated demand (LinkedIn, MBA groups), high willingness to pay
- Our seed catalog (Sapiens, Zero to One, Atomic Habits, Psychology of Money) already fits
- Don't expand until this works. Cambly owned "spontaneous English conversation" before Kids/Business.
- **Target: 100 consumers, 50 sessions, ready for Product Hunt launch**

### GTM channels by priority:
1. LinkedIn organic (founders, PMs, MBA students)
2. Reddit communities (book + professional subs)
3. WhatsApp/Telegram groups (India-specific distribution)
4. Book club partnerships
5. Twitter/X (book recommendation threads)
6. Product Hunt (after 50+ sessions with real reviews)

### Total cost: $0
Everything above is founder hustle. No paid marketing until product-market fit is proven.

## Additional Questions Investors Will Ask

### 7. What's the market size?

- **TAM:** Global book market ($140B) + online education market ($350B) — Vyaz sits at the intersection
- **SAM:** English-language book knowledge consumers willing to pay for time savings — ~$5B (30M Blinkist users × $100/year + growth)
- **SOM (Year 1):** India-focused, business books — ~$10M addressable (100K potential users × $100/year)
- **Comparable exits:** Blinkist acquired for $160M (2022). Cambly valued at $1B+ (2023). Audible acquired by Amazon for $300M (2008, now does $1B+ revenue).

### 8. What's the business model?

- **Phase 1 (now):** Free — validate demand, build supply
- **Phase 2 (after PMF):** Commission per session — listener pays ₹299-999 per 30-min session, narrator keeps 70%, platform takes 30%
- **Phase 3 (scale):** Subscription — ₹999/month for unlimited sessions (like Cambly's model). Higher LTV, better retention.
- **Phase 4 (expansion):** B2B — corporate L&D licenses. Companies pay for employee access to book narrations. Higher ARPU, longer contracts.
- **Long-term:** Narrator certification + premium tiers. Verified narrators charge more. Author narrations at premium pricing.

### 9. What's the competitive landscape?

| Competitor | What they do | Why Vyaz wins |
|---|---|---|
| **Blinkist** | Text/audio summaries | No interaction, no Q&A, no personalization. Summaries are being commoditized by AI. |
| **Audible** | Full audiobooks | Same time problem (6-8 hrs). No interaction. |
| **MasterClass** | Celebrity-taught courses | Pre-recorded, no interaction, $180/year, not book-specific. |
| **Headway** | Visual book summaries | Same as Blinkist — no human, no Q&A. |
| **Book clubs** | Group discussions | Scheduling nightmare, unstructured, no expertise guarantee. |
| **ChatGPT** | AI book summaries | Free but no human perspective, no debate, no personalization beyond prompting. |

**Vyaz's moat:** The only platform where you can have a live, interactive, personalized conversation about a specific book with a verified human who has deeply read it. AI commoditizes summaries — Vyaz offers the premium human layer.

### 10. What are the key risks?

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Narrator quality** | Verification quiz per book, ratings/reviews, featured narrator system |
| **Supply-demand imbalance** | Start with founder-as-narrator, recruit narrowly for proven demand |
| **Disintermediation** (narrator takes clients off-platform) | Build reputation/ratings that don't transfer, offer payment processing, make the platform more valuable than going direct |
| **AI disruption** (AI gets good enough to replace narrators) | AI commoditizes summaries, not conversations. The interactive, Socratic, contextual Q&A is the moat — AI can't replicate genuine human perspective shaped by experience |
| **Retention** | Chapter progression (Netflix effect), takeaway cards, narrator relationships |
