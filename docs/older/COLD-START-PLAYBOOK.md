# Vyaz — Cold Start Playbook

*How to get the first 100 users when you have zero.*

---

## The Chicken-and-Egg Problem

Consumers won't come without narrators. Narrators won't stay without consumers. You need to break the loop — and the answer from the Cambly playbook is clear: **solve supply first, and be the supply yourself until it's real.**

---

## Phase 0: You Are the Product (Week 1-2)

**You (the founder) are the first narrator.** This is already set up — you're linked to 5 books on Vyaz. But you need to go harder:

- Add yourself as narrator for **15-20 books** you genuinely know well
- Set real availability (not mock) — 2 sessions per day, 5 days a week
- **Take every single session personally** for the first 2-4 weeks
- Record (with permission) 3-5 sessions as demo content

**Why this works:** You sidestep the marketplace problem entirely. You don't need narrator supply if you ARE the supply. You also learn exactly what consumers want by being in the room with them.

**Goal:** 10 completed sessions in 2 weeks, personally hosted by you.

---

## Phase 1: Recruit 10 Narrators (Week 2-4)

Don't recruit randomly. Target people who already discuss books for free:

### Where to find them

| Source | Why they'll say yes | How to reach them |
|---|---|---|
| **Your friends who read** | Personal ask, they'll do it as a favor | WhatsApp/call directly, "I built this, can you host 2 sessions?" |
| **Book club members** | They already discuss books weekly — this is what they do | Find 3-5 local/online book clubs, attend, pitch after |
| **Goodreads power reviewers** | They've written 500+ reviews, they clearly love talking about books | DM the top reviewers of your 20 seed books, offer featured placement |
| **BookTok/BookTube creators** | They narrate books on video already — this is a new channel | DM 20 creators with 1K-50K followers, offer "first narrator" status |
| **MBA students** | They read case studies and business books constantly | Post in MBA WhatsApp groups, LinkedIn MBA communities |
| **College professors** | They literally teach books for a living | Email 10 professors who teach courses based on your seed books |

### The pitch

> "I built a platform where people pay to talk to someone who's read a book they want to understand. You've read [Book X] — would you host 2-3 free sessions to help me test it? If it works, you'd be one of the first narrators when we launch paid sessions."

**Don't promise money yet.** The first 10 narrators should come for: curiosity, the founder asking personally, and the promise of being first.

**Goal:** 10 narrators covering 30+ unique titles.

---

## Phase 2: Get the First 50 Consumers (Week 3-6)

Now you have 10+ narrators. You need people who want to consume books but don't have time to read them. Target **urgency-driven** readers:

### Channel 1: LinkedIn (highest ROI for this product)

Post 3x/week:
- "I read [Book] so you don't have to. Here are the 3 ideas that changed how I think about X." (value post → CTA to Vyaz)
- "I talked to someone about [Book] for 30 minutes and understood it better than people who spent 8 hours reading it." (social proof)
- "Your reading list has 47 books on it. You'll never finish them. Here's what I did instead." (pain point → solution)

**Target audience:** Founders, PMs, consultants, MBA students — people who feel guilty about not reading enough.

### Channel 2: Reddit

Subreddits with your exact audience:
- r/books (22M members) — "I built a platform where you talk to someone who's read the book instead of reading it yourself"
- r/suggestmeabook (3M) — answer recommendation requests with "I can connect you with someone who's read this"
- r/Entrepreneur (2M) — "Instead of reading 'Zero to One', I talked to a founder who'd internalized it"
- r/getdisciplined (1M) — "I couldn't finish Atomic Habits. So I talked to someone who did."

**Don't spam.** Provide genuine value in comments, then mention Vyaz naturally.

### Channel 3: WhatsApp/Telegram Groups

You're in India — this is where your first users live:
- Startup founder groups
- MBA batch groups (IIMs, ISB, XLRI)
- Reading/book club groups
- Professional development groups

**The message:**
> "Hey, I built something for people like us who buy books and never finish them. You can talk to someone who's read the book for 30 minutes instead. It's free right now — trying to get feedback. [link]"

### Channel 4: Twitter/X

- Quote-tweet popular book recommendations with "Or you could just talk to someone who's read it → [link]"
- Reply to "what book should I read" threads
- Post session clips (30-second takeaway from a narration)

### Channel 5: Book Club Partnerships

- Find 5 online book clubs (Goodreads groups, Discord servers, Facebook groups)
- Offer: "Your members who didn't finish this month's book can catch up in 30 minutes with one of our narrators"
- This solves a real pain point every book club has — members who show up without reading

**Goal:** 50 registered consumers, 20 completed sessions.

---

## Phase 3: Create Social Proof (Week 4-8)

You need proof that this works before you can scale:

### Collect and publish
- **Session recordings** (with permission) → 60-second clips for LinkedIn/Twitter
- **Reviews from consumers** → screenshot and share
- **Narrator testimonials** → "I explained Sapiens to 5 people this week and loved every session"
- **Numbers** → "47 sessions completed. 4.8 average rating. 12 narrators."

### The content flywheel
```
Host a session → Record a clip → Post on LinkedIn
     ↑                                    ↓
More sessions ← New users sign up ← Post goes viral
```

One great session clip that goes viral on LinkedIn could bring 100+ signups overnight. This is your highest-leverage activity.

---

## Phase 4: The Wedge (Week 6-12)

Don't try to be "a platform for all books." Own ONE narrow wedge first:

### Best wedge for Vyaz: Business/startup books for Indian professionals

Why:
- **High willingness to pay** — professionals invest in learning
- **Time-scarce audience** — they genuinely can't read 4 hours/day
- **Concentrated distribution** — LinkedIn, MBA groups, startup communities
- **Your seed catalog fits** — Sapiens, Zero to One, Atomic Habits, Psychology of Money are all in this lane
- **Narrators are available** — MBA students, founders, consultants have read these books

Own this wedge before expanding to fiction, self-help, academic books, etc. Cambly owned "spontaneous English conversation" before touching Kids/Business — same principle.

---

## What NOT to Do

1. **Don't build more features** — you have enough product. The bottleneck is distribution, not features.
2. **Don't run paid ads** — at this stage, every user should come from a personal touchpoint or organic content.
3. **Don't recruit 50 narrators** — 10 good ones covering 30 titles is better than 50 covering 200 with no demand.
4. **Don't launch on Product Hunt yet** — wait until you have 50+ completed sessions and real reviews.
5. **Don't price it yet** — keep it free until you have proof that people show up and come back.

## The First 100 Users — Summary

| Week | Action | Target |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Founder hosts all sessions personally | 10 sessions |
| 2-4 | Recruit 10 narrators from personal network + book communities | 10 narrators, 30 titles |
| 3-6 | LinkedIn posts, Reddit, WhatsApp groups, book club partnerships | 50 consumers, 20 sessions |
| 4-8 | Publish session clips, reviews, testimonials | Social proof loop |
| 6-12 | Double down on business books for Indian professionals | 100 consumers, 50 sessions |

**Total cost: $0.** Everything above is founder hustle, not paid marketing.

---

## When to Know It's Working

- **Retention signal:** A consumer books a second session (different book, same narrator or different narrator)
- **Supply signal:** A narrator asks "when will I get more bookings?" (they want demand, not the other way around)
- **Viral signal:** A consumer shares Vyaz with a friend unprompted
- **Pricing signal:** Someone asks "how much does this cost?" before you've set a price

When you see 2+ of these signals, you've solved cold start. Then you raise, hire, and scale.
