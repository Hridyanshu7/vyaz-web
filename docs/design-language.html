01

The thesis

Vyaz exists to carry an author's original thinking to a reader — faithfully, and in the way humans have always passed knowledge on: in conversation. Everything in this document defends that idea. Nothing about that changed in v2 — only how it looks.

Named for Vyasa — the sage who spoke the Mahabharata and the Puranas into being — Vyaz treats the person who put pen to paper as the source of truth, and the voice as a faithful medium, never a substitute. In an era of AI slop that averages everything into sludge, Vyaz is a stand for the opposite: the genuine, the sourced, the whole.

01

Fidelity over slop

We deliver the author's actual words — verbatim. The AI narrates; it never paraphrases, summarizes, or invents. Faithfulness is the product, not a setting.

02

Conversation is oldest

Knowledge moves the way we evolved to move it — spoken, in dialogue you can interrupt and question. The epics were spoken before they were written.

03

Honor the author

The human who created the idea is named, credited, and rewarded. Vyaz elevates originators, it doesn't launder them into anonymity.

04

Rooted, then modern

Indian intellectual heritage in the name and the mark; everywhere else, the product should feel like the best modern software our users already use daily.

The one line

If a design choice makes Vyaz feel like generated content rather than a real person's genuine work, faithfully carried — it's wrong, however pretty. And if it makes Vyaz feel like a dated regional app rather than the sharpest product our users have on their phone — it's also wrong.

02

Personality & voice

Vyaz is a warm, encouraging tutor with real substance — a patient mentor who happens to be deeply well-read. Warm, never saccharine. Confident, never arrogant. Plain-spoken, never dumbed-down.

We are

Warm · patient · precise · respectful of both the author and the learner · quietly confident · unhurried.

We are not

Hype-y · salesy · jargon-heavy · gamified · ironic · anything that smells of the AI slop we exist to oppose.

Voice & tone in practice

Write like this

"Let's pick up where we left off."

"Narrated from Ben Horowitz's own words."

"That chapter didn't load. Tap to try again."

Plain, human, specific. Credit the author by name. Errors say what happened and the way out.

Not like this

"🚀 Supercharge your learning journey!"

"Our AI summarized this book for you."

"Oops! Something went wrong 😬"

Hype, emoji confetti, and — worst of all — implying we summarized or replaced the author.

The tell: our language always keeps the author present. We never say Vyaz "knows" or "wrote" — Vyaz carries, narrates, reads. The credit points outward, to the human.

03

The name & the seal

Vyaz → Vyasa: the archetypal narrator-compiler of Indian knowledge. The name carries the whole thesis in four letters — the trusted voice that faithfully delivers the great works.

The mark: kept, but demoted from "the whole palette" to "one detail"

v1 made the seal — a red hanko/chhāp-style stamp bearing the Devanagari va (व) — the app's dominant accent color, used sparingly but still as the single interactive color everywhere. In practice that made the product read as severe and stamp-like rather than premium, and sat too close to older Indian reading platforms' visual habits.

v2 keeps the glyph and the idea of a seal of authenticity, but retires its old red role as a dominant, everywhere-color: the logo mark itself now carries the signature gradient (§5) — a decorative, expressive fill, since a static logo is exactly where a brand is allowed a flourish. It no longer governs buttons, links, or any other interactive color. The glyph itself is a placeholder for now — a fuller visual refresh of the mark is a tracked follow-up, not part of this pass.

व

The mark, still red-adjacent through the gradient, still meaning "the real, sourced original" — the only place the old seal red's authenticity idea appears at all now.

Flat --color-seal has no active use in the current UI. v1/early-v2 also used it for a "Live" status dot in the Session Modal — that dot is gone (§12): liveness is now communicated by the voice orb's own state colors (accent/sage), not a separate red indicator. The token stays defined, reserved for a plausible future use (an author-verification badge, say) rather than forced into a role it no longer needs to fill.

04

Positioning & who we're not

Vyaz is a premium AI study companion for serious learners in India — students, aspirants, and professionals who want depth, not shortcuts. Our users are fluent in LinkedIn, YouTube, Instagram, and WhatsApp, in that order — they know what a well-made product looks like, and they compare us to it, not to a "reading app."

We are

A faithful narrator of real books, in conversation.

A place that credits and rewards authors.

As polished as the modern SaaS/AI products our users already use daily — Linear, Notion, Perplexity, ElevenLabs.

We are not

A summary/"book-in-15-min" app.

A generic AI chatbot that answers about books from its own training.

Visually interchangeable with dated regional reading platforms (Pratilipi, Hubhawks) — that comparison is the thing this document exists to prevent.

The accent rule (this is the v1→v2 reversal)

v1 said "spend the seal like it costs money." v2 says the opposite for the accent: use it as the default primary action color everywhere — every main CTA, every selected/active state, every focus ring, every "this is the important thing on this screen" moment. That's how Linear, Notion, and Perplexity actually use their brand color, and it's why their products feel confident rather than rationed. The seal red is what stays out of the rotation entirely — reserved (§3), not deployed as a status color anymore.

The signature gradient — a brand moment, not a UI system

Flat color carries the product; one gradient carries the personality. --gradient-signal is a cool-to-warm diagonal ramp — indigo → teal → gold — built so it can't be mistaken for a generic "app icon rainbow": no magenta or pink anywhere in it, no orange finish, and it deliberately passes through teal, a hue most consumer-app gradients skip entirely. It reads as ink settling into voice — cool and written, then warm and spoken — which is the product's actual thesis, not a decorative accident.

It is used in exactly four places, and nowhere else:

  1. The व logo mark — a decorative fill on a static symbol (§3).
  2. One ambient hero glow behind the Home page's headline/CTA — blurred, low-opacity, a light source, not a shape.
  3. An ambient glow behind the book cover during an active Talk session — again a soft blur behind the art, never a hard-edged ring or stroke drawn around it (a colored ring around a thumbnail is a specific, recognizable UI affordance from other consumer apps that we deliberately avoid — ours is a glow, not a ring).
  4. The Talk/voice CTA itself — at most one per screen (§11) — because that button is the product's defining gesture, functionally the same category as the logo. Every other primary button stays flat.

The gradient rule

The signature gradient is decorative, not semantic — with exactly one deliberate exception (the single Talk CTA, §11), it never appears on a badge, a status dot, a link, or anything a user needs to read at a glance to know what happened. Status and semantic meaning always stay flat (accent, seal, rating, sage/ochre/oxblood). If a gradient would ever need to double as a "this means X" signal — success, error, selected, disabled — that's the sign it's being misused.

Star ratings get their own color

Ratings (Goodreads/Amazon-style stars shown on every book) use --color-rating, a warm amber — not the brand accent, not the seal. Readers already read gold stars as "rating" everywhere else; fighting that convention to force brand consistency isn't worth the confusion.

Semantic states vs. the seal vs. the accent

Error red (oxblood) is deliberately a different, browner red than the seal, so that if the seal is ever reactivated for an authenticity badge, "something's wrong" can never get confused with "this is genuine." The accent never doubles as an error or warning color either — three distinct reds/colors (seal, error, accent) that never get to stand in for each other, even though only two of them are actually in use right now.

Dark mode: intentionally out of scope for this pass — not forgotten, just sequenced later (see §13). These tokens are defined as plain CSS custom properties so adding a dark variant later is additive, not a rewrite.

Brand typeface

Adopt Geist (body/display) and Geist Mono (data/labels) — both self-hosted, both free, both read as "the modern AI product our users already use," which Inter no longer signals now that it's the default everywhere. No serif anywhere in the product. Fallbacks: system-ui/ui-sans-serif/ui-monospace.

Type scale & rules

  • Scale: 12 · 13 · 14 · 16 (body) · 20 · 24 · 32 · 40 · 56. Stay on it.
  • Headings: sans, weight 700, tight leading (~1.1), text-wrap: balance, slightly tightened letter-spacing at large sizes.
  • Body: sans, weight 400, leading ~1.6, line length ≤ 68 characters.
  • Labels/eyebrows: mono, uppercase, letter-spacing ~0.1em, in stone or accent-deep — this narrow "mono for data/labels" convention already existed in a few places in the app; it's now a documented rule, not an accident.
  • Numerals in tables/timers use tabular-nums.

09

Icon, imagery & the mark

  • Icons: line icons, ~1.5px stroke (Lucide, already in use — keep it). Icons pick up the accent only when they express an active/selected/live state, otherwise ink/stone.
  • Imagery: real book covers, clean product screenshots, and honest UI — no manuscript/brushwork textures, no stocky 3D-gradient "AI" glow, no generic illustration. If it wouldn't look at home on Linear or Perplexity's marketing site, it doesn't go on ours.
  • The mark carries the signature gradient (logo only); the live-indicator stays flat seal red — see §3.
  • No colored rings or strokes around thumbnails/avatars to signal "active"/"live" — use an ambient blurred glow behind the element instead. This is a firm rule, not a style preference: a drawn ring around a small circular or rectangular thumbnail is a specific, recognizable affordance elsewhere in consumer apps, and we don't want any part of the product read as an homage to it.
  • Author presence: wherever a book appears, the author's name is visible and dignified — never buried. This didn't change.
10

Motion

Motion is still purposeful, but v2 allows a bit more life than v1's "earn every motion" austerity — a hover-lift on a card, a smooth accent glow on focus, are cheap ways to feel alive without becoming decoration for its own sake.

  • Durations: 150–250ms for UI transitions; ease-out for entrances.
  • Hover-lift: cards and clickable rows may lift 1–2px with shadow-raised on hover — a standard, restrained "this is clickable" cue.
  • Meaningful motion: the voice orb's waveform, the reconnect countdown, a gentle fade as transcript streams in — still the core budget, just no longer the only motion allowed.
  • Always honor prefers-reduced-motion.
11

Components in the language

Buttons — two tiers, not one

The accent is the default primary everywhere — reversing v1's "ink primary, seal reserved" rule. But one specific button carries more: the Talk/voice CTA, at most one per screen, is the product's defining gesture — a 4th rationed use of the signature gradient (§5), echoing v1's old "the seal button is reserved for the single most brand-defining action" instinct, just expressed through the gradient instead of flat seal-red. Every other primary action — Save, Continue, Get started — stays flat indigo, on purpose, so the one gradient button still stands out as *the* one.

Ordinary primary actions — flat, quiet, everywhere

The one signature action — icon-only, no label

The Talk CTA carries no visible copy at all — not "Talk to this book," not "Play." A first pass nested a play triangle inside a speech-bubble, but a play glyph is inescapably media-playback-coded, no matter what shape you put it in — it undersells the point (you can interrupt and ask it things — this isn't a podcast). The icon that actually fits is a plain mic: it's the single most familiar "tap to start talking" convention across voice apps generically (not an homage to any one product, the way a specific gradient recipe or a ring-around-a-thumbnail would be) — and it's not an arbitrary pick, either: GeminiLiveModal already shows this exact mic glyph, pulsing, the moment it's the user's turn to speak mid-session. The launch button is now a preview of a state the product already has, not a new symbol invented for the occasion.

Icon-only ≠ unlabeled: visually there's no text, but the button still carries a real accessible name (aria-label="Talk to this book") for screen readers — see §12. Dropping the visible label doesn't mean dropping the promise, just the words.

The Session Modal

This is the product. Everything else in this document is packaging around the few minutes someone spends actually talking to a book — so nothing here is decorative. Every color and icon on this screen carries real, live meaning: is the agent talking, is it your turn, where are we in the book, did the connection just drop. Reskin the colors, never blur the distinctions.

This section reflects the redesigned structure validated in the block-lab prototype (talk/TalkLayout.jsx), combined with the features already shipped in production that block-lab hadn't ported yet — thumbs feedback and the rating screen. Treated here as the settled direction, not a rough sketch.

Shell & layout — one control, not a bar of buttons

Two big changes from the old structure. First: verbatim narration no longer lives in the chat at all — it lives on the book's own real page, word-highlighted as it's read. Second: there is no separate Live tag, End Session button, or waveform strip — all three collapse into one circular control, the voice orb. It's always visible, it's how the agent is summoned, and it's the only control surface in the whole modal: once live, any command — end the session, jump to a chapter, ask a question — is just spoken to it. No hunting for the right button.

  • Header: just the book title + chapter name/number (mono eyebrow). Nothing else competes for space here anymore.
  • The voice orb — a small (~52px) circle that floats in the bottom-right corner, overlapping the content slightly, the way a call app's controls or a chat widget's compose button do — it never claims a full-width row of its own. Waveform bars inside it, colored by who's talking (accent = agent, sage = you), a plain mic glyph while idle/connecting. No visible text label — color, icon, and animation carry the state; a screen-reader-only announcement covers accessibility instead of a persistent on-screen pill. Tap it to engage; everything past that point is spoken, not tapped.
  • Two panels — Conversation (~45%, left): the agent's own asides and the reader's spoken questions/answers, nothing else. The Book (~55%, right): the real, fully-formatted chapter — headings, paragraphs, lists, callouts, tables, images — with the currently-read block highlighted word by word.
  • Whole-book seek docked below both panels — one continuous track for the entire book, not just the current chapter. Still clickable directly, but "skip to chapter 5" spoken to the orb does the same thing.

Below sm, the two panels collapse into a Chat / Book tab switch rather than a vertical stack — each view earns the full screen width on a phone. A session now connects immediately in portrait, exactly like desktop — no rotate prompt, no landscape requirement. That gate existed only because the old two-column layout needed desktop-like width to be usable; the Chat/Book tabs were built specifically to work in portrait, so the gate no longer has a reason to exist and has been dropped.

The Contrarian Question

Silicon Valley has become synonymous with a certain relentless optimism about the future. Every year, a new crop of ambitious college dropouts convince investors to bet millions on their vision of what's next.

Whenever I interview someone for a job, I ask: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" This question sounds easy because it's straightforward. It's actually very hard to answer.

The best way to predict the future is to invent it — and that requires thinking from first principles about what's actually possible, not just extrapolating from what's come before.

Every moment in business happens only once. The next Bill Gates will not build an operating system. The next Larry Page or Sergey Brin won't make a search engine.

If you are copying these men, you aren't learning from them. Of course, it's easier to copy a model than to make something new.

The Book panel — where verbatim fidelity actually lives now

This is the change that matters most for the thesis (§1). Book-verbatim text is no longer paraphrased into a chat bubble at all — it's the author's real, fully-formatted page, and the currently-read block gets a word-by-word "karaoke" reveal: already-read words full opacity, not-yet-read words dimmed, wrapped in an accent left-border + tint so the active paragraph is unmistakable. Already-covered blocks settle to a quiet 55% opacity; blocks still ahead sit at 35%.

Non-negotiable

The reader must always be able to see, at a glance, the exact real page the narrator is reading from — not a paraphrase of it, not a chat bubble standing in for it. Putting verbatim text directly on the book's own formatted content, rather than inside a chat bubble, is a stronger expression of the anti-slop thesis than the old design, not just a different layout — there is no ambiguity left about whether these are the author's words.

The Conversation panel, correspondingly, only ever shows two things: the agent's own asides (italic, ink-soft, thin left border — quieter than a bubble, since it's commentary, not content) and the reader's own spoken turns (a proper bubble, canvas-tinted, never accent or seal).

The voice orb — one control, three states, color is state not decoration

The orb replaces a whole bar of separate parts (Live tag, End Session button, waveform strip) with one circular control that's always doing one of three things. This mapping is fixed, not a palette choice to swap freely:

Speaking — narrator is talking (accent fill) Listening — it's your turn (sage fill) Idle / connecting — bordered, neutral, plain mic glyph

Idle shows the exact same mic glyph as the launch CTA (§11) — press the mic on the book's page, and the orb you land in shows you that same icon before the session connects. Once live, the icon gives way to animated waveform bars, and the orb's own fill color is the whole visible signal for who's talking — no on-screen text label sits beside it. Accessibility is still covered (color alone can't be the only signal, §13), just via a screen-reader-only, aria-live state announcement rather than a persistent visible pill nobody sighted needs.

Any command, one control

There is no dedicated "End Session" or "change chapter" button anywhere in this modal anymore. Both are ordinary spoken requests, handled by the agent through the orb, exactly like asking a question. Ending a session hands off to the rating screen exactly as before (§ End of session, below); changing chapters moves the same whole-book seek position a click on the seek bar would.

Whole-book seek — replaces the old per-chapter progress list

One continuous track for the entire book, not just the open chapter: a dot per chapter, sized by cumulative word count, filled accent showing overall position. Clicking a dot jumps to the start of that chapter (so does asking the orb) — it does not scrub mid-chapter, since a live Gemini audio stream can't jump mid-stream without re-prompting the model. This is a deliberate downgrade in precision (no more "3 of 5 sections done" within a chapter) in exchange for a much more honest whole-book sense of place, which the old per-chapter list never gave at all.

Per-message feedback

Thumbs up/down now live under the agent's asides and Q&A answers specifically — not under verbatim narration, since narration no longer renders as a message at all. Quiet by default, colored only once pressed (sage / oxblood); a thumbs-down reveals an inline remarks field that commits only on explicit submit, never autosaves on keystroke.

Reconnecting

Long chapters outlive a single WebSocket; session resumption reconnects automatically. This is exactly the kind of motion the Motion principle (§10) calls "earned" — it explains real state, so it's allowed to be a little more present than the baseline restraint.

End of session — the rating screen

Every session ends with a mandatory 1–5★ rating plus optional free text. Stars use --color-rating (amber) — never the accent, never the seal — and the submit button is an ordinary primary (flat indigo), not the signature gradient: the gradient is spent once, at launch (§5, §11); it doesn't reappear at the exit.

Accessibility baseline

  • Contrast: body text meets WCAG AA (4.5:1). Ink-on-surface clears it easily; use accent-deep, not accent, when the brand color must be small text/links.
  • Focus: every interactive element has a visible accent focus ring (shadow-glow); never remove outlines without a replacement.
  • Motion: respect prefers-reduced-motion; nothing critical is conveyed by motion or color alone.
  • Targets: touch targets ≥ 44×44px. Voice controls especially — thumbs, on phones, mid-listen.
  • Color-only meaning: the three "on" colors that must never be confused — accent (brand/selected), seal (live/authentic), rating (stars) — are checked for mutual distinctness, not just contrast against the background.
  • Icon-only buttons (the Talk CTA, §11) always carry a real accessible name (aria-label or equivalent) even with zero visible text — the label may be dropped from the eye, never from assistive tech.

How this document governs

This is the baseline. New screens, features, and marketing derive from it — they don't reinvent it.

Precedence when choices conflict
  1. The thesis (§1) — does it honor the author, the anti-slop stand, and read as premium/modern to our actual audience?
  2. These tokens (§5–§8) — color, type, space, shape.
  3. Component convention — reuse existing patterns before inventing.
Source of truth & changes
  • Tokens live in the app's Tailwind theme (v4 @theme); the names in §5 are the contract. Design and code share one set of variables.
  • A change to a token or a brand rule is a decision — log it in docs/DECISIONS.md (as an ADR) so the "why" survives, exactly like engineering choices. This v1→v2 rewrite itself is logged there.
  • When a real need contradicts this doc, that's a signal to update the doc, not to quietly diverge.
Explicitly deferred, not forgotten
  • Dark mode — out of scope for v2. Tokens are structured as plain CSS custom properties so a dark variant can be additive later.
  • The seal glyph itself — kept as a placeholder mark for now; a fuller visual redesign of the mark is a tracked follow-up, not part of this pass.
Remember

Every pixel is arguing for the same thing the product is: the genuine article, faithfully carried, delivered as sharp as the best software our users already have on their phones.
